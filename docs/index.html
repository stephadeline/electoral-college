How does the electoral college work?


The Electoral College is made of 538 electors that determines which presidential candidate wins, who must win a majority to gain the title.

But when voters submit their polls, they are only choosing which candidate will receive all of the state’s electors, which can vary depending on the state. The candidates must win the state’s popular vote to take all of its electoral votes. And if they win at least 270 of the electoral votes across the nation, they win the presidency.

Why was the Electoral College created?


Founding fathers first created the Electoral College for two reasons:

  1. They feared that smaller states would not receive fair representation

  2. They argued that common man were not informed enough to choose a suitable president.


What happens if no candidate receives a majority of the Electoral College votes?


Although the United States is usually divided among the Democrats and the Republicans, sometimes there are third parties. In this case, if no one gets a majority of the electoral votes, the election is sent to the U.S. House of Representatives. Whoever wins a majority of states wins the election.


Why is the Electoral College so controversial?


There have been five instances (such as the recent 2016 election) when the votes of the electoral college differ from the popular votes result. This is because some states have more vote than others, depending on their population.

Presidential candidates in the past have been able to lose the election with a majority of the popular vote. These outcomes has made the Electoral College subject to criticism.
